What if dark matter is not hanging around as individual particles, but rather as a composite state, like a Standard Model nucleus? With simple ingredients (DM particles and an attractive force between them), DM composite states are formed in the early universe. The phenomenology of their detection is rich, however -- from pointline scattering to scattering with constituents.

![A plot of the composite parameter space and corresponding scattering regimes.](/compositeparameterspace.png)

<!--more-->

In this work, [Loosely bound composite dark matter]("https://doi.org/10.1088/1475-7516/2025/03/013"), we explore the detectable signatures of composite dark matter states, formed of heavy WIMPs bound by an attractive force. We outline the direct detection prospects of ``loosely bound" composites, which have a small binding energy per dark nucleon relative to their constituent mass. This work demonstrated that loosely bound composites can scatter multiple times in a single passage through a detector. This is a distinctive signature to which direct detection experiments are sensitive; indeed, various experimental collaborations have recently published results from their multi-scattering dark matter searches. This work provides the first link between this multi-scattering signature and a concrete, well-motivated dark matter candidate. It also showed that loosely bound composites can cause numerous very low-energy scatters, a novel signature in low-threshold silicon detectors, which are generally seeking low-mass dark matter. This could allow these low-threshold experiments to constrain high-mass dark matter as well.
