In the extremely loosely bound regime of composite dark matter, the constituents are easily ejected from a composite by scattering with Standard Model nuclei. If such a composite passes through the Earth, the constituents may be completely disassembled before they reach the other side. What are the detection prospects of such a "cloud" of constituents?

![A sketch of a DM composite disassembling in the Earth.](/DisassemblySketch.png)

<!--more-->

In this work, [Deconstructive Composite Dark Matter Detection]("https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.16043v2"), we investigate the detection of composite dark matter that disassembles into a cascade while crossing the Earth. This occurs for loosely bound composite dark matter, where the binding energy per constituent is small, such that scattering with Standard Model nuclei typically imparts enough energy to dissociate a constituent from its composite. Trajectories and cascade profiles are found for dissociated constituents that are further diverted by scattering through the Earth. Such scattering cascades are a common feature of TeV-scale weakly-interacting dark matter loosely bound in composites. We identify underground detector signatures of constituent cascades that depend on composite characteristics; these signatures include non-collinear multiple scatters in detectors, parameter-dependent timing separation of multiscatter events, and regions of parameter space where a dark matter cascade would leave a coincident signature in different underground laboratories.
